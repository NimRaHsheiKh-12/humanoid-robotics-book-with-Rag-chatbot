# Chapter 2: Robotics Anatomy

## Joints, Actuators, and Sensors

Humanoid robots are intricate machines, and their "anatomy" is a marvel of engineering that mimics, to some extent, biological systems. The fundamental components enabling their movement and interaction are joints, actuators, and sensors.

*   **Joints**: These are the connection points between different body segments of the robot, analogous to human joints. They allow for rotational or translational motion, providing the robot with flexibility and a range of motion. Common types include revolute (like a hinge) and prismatic (linear sliding) joints.
*   **Actuators**: These are the "muscles" of the robot. Actuators convert energy (typically electrical, hydraulic, or pneumatic) into mechanical force to cause motion. Electric motors are prevalent in smaller humanoid robots, while hydraulic systems are used for larger, more powerful movements.
*   **Sensors**: These are the robot's "senses," providing feedback about its internal state and the external environment. Examples include:
    *   **Proprioceptive sensors**: Measure the robot's own state, such as joint angles, motor speeds, and forces applied at various points (e.g., encoders, potentiometers, force-torque sensors).
    *   **Exteroceptive sensors**: Gather information about the robot's surroundings, such as cameras (vision), LiDAR (distance and mapping), microphones (hearing), and tactile sensors (touch).

## Human-like vs. Functional Design

The design of humanoid robots often oscillates between aspiring to human likeness and optimizing for specific functionalities.

*   **Human-like Design**: Robots like ASIMO or Ameca aim for aesthetic and kinematic similarity to humans. This design facilitates interaction in human-centric environments and can leverage existing tools and infrastructure. However, replicating human complexity can be mechanically challenging and energy-intensive.
*   **Functional Design**: Some robots prioritize functionality over strict human resemblance. For instance, a robot designed primarily for heavy lifting might have an exaggerated upper body strength or a unique joint configuration that isn't biologically accurate but is highly efficient for its task. The choice depends on the robot's intended role: for social interaction, human-like appearance is beneficial; for industrial tasks, pure efficiency might be paramount.
